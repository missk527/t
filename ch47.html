Rates of Reaction from Graphs — Questions & Answers

  1. What are the two ways we can calculate the rate of a reaction?
    → From the amount of reactant used or the amount of product formed.
  2. What is the general formula for rate of reaction?
    → Rate = change in amount ÷ time.
  3. In the example, how much hydrogen gas was produced?
    → 1,200 cm³.
  4. How long did the reaction take in the example?
    → 4 minutes.
  5. What was the calculated mean rate of the reaction in cm³/s?
    → 5 cm³/s.
  6. Why does this calculation only give the average rate?
    → Because the rate changes during the reaction.
  7. When is the rate of reaction fastest?
    → At the beginning.
  8. What happens to the rate of reaction as the reaction progresses?
    → It slows down.
  9. On a reaction graph, what is plotted on the x-axis?
    → Time.
  10. On a reaction graph, what is plotted on the y-axis?
    → Volume of hydrogen (or product formed).
  11. What does a steep curve indicate on the graph?
    → A fast rate of reaction.
  12. What does a plateau on the graph show?
    → The reaction has finished.
  13. What causes the reaction to slow down over time?
    → Reactants are being used up.
  14. What two main calculations can be done from a reaction graph?
    → Mean rate and rate at a specific time.
  15. What does “mean rate of reaction” mean?
    → The average rate over a time period.
  16. How do you find the mean rate for the first 3 minutes?
    → Find the volume at 3 minutes and divide by time in seconds.
  17. In the example, how much hydrogen was produced in 3 minutes?
    → 1,200 cm³.
  18. What was the mean rate for the first 3 minutes?
    → 6.67 cm³/s.
  19. What does “rate at a specific time” mean?
    → The rate at one instant during the reaction.
  20. What do we need to draw to find the rate at a specific time?
    → A tangent.
  21. What is a tangent?
    → A straight line that just touches the curve at one point.
  22. How is the tangent related to the curve?
    → It has the same gradient as the curve at that point.
  23. What does the gradient of the tangent represent?
    → The rate of reaction at that moment.
  24. What formula is used to calculate the gradient?
    → Gradient = Δy ÷ Δx.
  25. What does Δy represent in this context?
    → The change in volume of hydrogen.
  26. What does Δx represent in this context?
    → The change in time.
  27. What was the change in hydrogen volume used in the tangent example?
    → 600 cm³.
  28. What was the change in time used in the tangent example?
    → 170 seconds (2 minutes 50 seconds).
  29. What was the calculated rate at 2 minutes in cm³/s?
    → 3.53 cm³/s.
  30. Why is it recommended to make the tangent line long?
    → To reduce percentage error in measurement.
  31. What should you do after drawing the tangent line?
    → Measure Δy and Δx to calculate the gradient.
  32. Why do examiners allow a small range of answers?
    → Because the tangent is drawn by eye.
  33. What should you be careful about when drawing the tangent?
    → Make sure it matches the curve’s steepness at that point.
  34. What type of graph can also be used to find reaction rates besides product-time graphs?
    → A graph of reactant remaining vs time.
  35. In the magnesium example, what was the starting mass?
    → 1.2 g.
  36. What was the change in mass (Δy) at 1 minute?
    → 0.72 g.
  37. What was the change in time (Δx) at 1 minute?
    → 100 seconds (1 minute 40 seconds).
  38. What was the calculated rate in g/s for that reaction?
    → 0.0072 g/s.
  39. Why is it okay if the tangent is a little longer or shorter?
    → The gradient stays the same if steepness is correct.
  40. What ensures you will get the same rate even if your tangent size differs?
    → Keeping the same slope (steepness) as the curve at that point.
