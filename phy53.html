Distance–Time Graphs — Questions & Answers

  1. What do distance–time graphs allow us to visualize?
    → How far something travels over time.
  2. What can a distance–time graph show about a journey?
    → The total distance, total time, and different stages of motion.
  3. In the example, how far did the cyclist travel in total?
    → 50 meters.
  4. How long did the cyclist’s journey take?
    → 11 seconds.
  5. What does each section of the graph represent?
    → A different part or stage of the journey.
  6. What does the gradient of the line tell us?
    → The speed of the object.
  7. What is the formula for speed?
    → Speed = distance ÷ time.
  8. How is the gradient calculated on a distance–time graph?
    → Change in distance ÷ change in time.
  9. What does gradient equal on a distance–time graph?
    → Speed.
  10. What is the gradient if an object travels 20 m in 2 seconds?
    → 10 m/s.
  11. What speed does 20 m in 2 seconds represent?
    → 10 meters per second (m/s).
  12. What does a straight line on a distance–time graph show?
    → Constant speed.
  13. What does a flat (horizontal) line mean?
    → The object is stationary.
  14. What is the speed when the gradient is zero?
    → Zero.
  15. What does it mean if the line becomes steeper?
    → The object is moving faster.
  16. What does an increasing gradient indicate?
    → Acceleration.
  17. What does a decreasing gradient indicate?
    → Deceleration.
  18. What term describes when the line gets less steep?
    → Deceleration.
  19. Why is it harder to find the speed on a curved part of the graph?
    → Because the speed is constantly changing.
  20. How can you find speed at a particular time on a curve?
    → Draw a tangent at that point and find its gradient.
  21. What is a tangent?
    → A straight line that just touches the curve at one point.
  22. How is a tangent related to the curve?
    → It has the same gradient as the curve at that point.
  23. What does the gradient of a tangent represent?
    → The instantaneous speed at that moment.
  24. What do you need to know to calculate the gradient?
    → The change in distance and change in time.
  25. What two quantities are divided to find the gradient?
    → Change in distance ÷ change in time.
  26. What is the change in distance in the example for the tangent?
    → 12 meters.
  27. What is the change in time in the tangent example?
    → 3 seconds.
  28. What speed is calculated from 12 m divided by 3 s?
    → 4 m/s.
  29. What does a curved line on a distance–time graph represent?
    → Changing speed.
  30. What do straight lines on a distance–time graph represent?
    → Constant speed.
  31. What do flat lines represent?
    → Stationary (no movement).
  32. What type of line shows acceleration?
    → A line that gets steeper.
  33. What type of line shows deceleration?
    → A line that becomes less steep.
  34. What must you calculate to find speed at a given point?
    → The gradient at that point.
  35. If the point lies on a straight line, how do you find speed?
    → Divide the total distance by the total time for that section.
  36. If the point lies on a curve, what extra step is needed?
    → Draw a tangent first, then find its gradient.
  37. What units are used for speed in the examples?
    → Meters per second (m/s).
  38. What happens to speed when the gradient increases?
    → Speed increases.
  39. What happens to speed when the gradient decreases?
    → Speed decreases.
  40. Summarize the rule connecting gradient and speed on a distance–time graph.
    → Gradient = Speed.
